# Best Chew Toys for Teething Puppies (2026)

The teething phase—an exciting but challenging time for you and your new puppy. Between the ages of 3 and 6 months, your puppy's baby teeth will fall out and adult teeth will emerge, causing discomfort, excessive chewing, and the urge to gnaw on everything in sight. Finding safe, effective chew toys is essential both to soothe your puppy's pain and to protect your furniture, shoes, and belongings.

The right chew toys can make a world of difference during this phase, providing relief from teething discomfort while promoting healthy chewing habits. In this guide, we've compiled the best options for teething puppies, with a focus on safety, durability, and effectiveness in soothing sore gums.

## What to Look for in Chew Toys for Teething Puppies

### Material Safety and Composition

The most important consideration is material safety. Avoid toys made with potentially harmful chemicals, PVC, BPA, or materials that could splinter. Natural rubber, food-grade silicone, and washable cotton are generally safe options. Be particularly careful with rope toys—look for natural fibers without dyes or treatments that could be harmful if swallowed. Always choose toys specifically designed for puppies, not adult dogs, as puppy toys are formulated with different safety standards.

### Appropriate Hardness and Texture

Ideally, choose toys with a soft-to-medium texture for the teething phase. Toys that are too hard can damage baby teeth (which are fragile) and can be painful on inflamed gums. However, toys that are too soft won't satisfy chewing urges and may be easily destroyed. The best toys provide enough resistance to make chewing satisfying while still being gentle enough for teething discomfort. Textured surfaces with bumps, ridges, or varying surfaces help massage gums and clean teeth.

### Size Appropriateness

Size matters both for safety and effectiveness. A toy that's too small can be a choking hazard, while one that's too large may be difficult for your puppy to grip or chew effectively. Consider your puppy's breed and size, and choose toys proportional to their mouth size. For growing puppies, you may need to upgrade to larger sizes as they grow, especially faster-growing large breeds that outgrow toys more quickly.

### Cleaning and Maintenance Considerations

Teething puppies often have sensitive immune systems, so regular cleaning of chew toys is essential. Look for toys that are easy to clean—dishwasher-safe hard toys, machine-washable rope toys (or at least easily washable), and surfaces that don't harbor bacteria. Avoid toys with crevices, nooks, or irregular surfaces that can trap drool, bacteria, and food particles. Establish a regular cleaning schedule (at least weekly, more for toys that get heavy use).

### Chewing Style and Breed Considerations

Different puppies have different chewing styles and intensities. Some puppies are gentle nibblers, while others are aggressive chewers who destroy toys quickly. Consider your puppy's chewing intensity when selecting toys—aggressive chewers need more durable options, while gentle chewers can do with softer toys. Breed size and jaw strength also affect appropriate toy selection. Toy breeds need smaller, softer toys, while large breeds may need larger, more substantial options.

### Multiple Toy Rotation Benefits

Having multiple chew toys on rotation offers several benefits. It provides variety to prevent boredom, allows some toys to rest while others are in use, and gives you options for different situations (e.g., one for freezing, one for treats, one for gentle evening chewing). Rotating toys also helps you identify which toys your puppy prefers and which are less effective. Keep a selection of 3-5 different types available for maximum effectiveness.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should I replace teething chew toys?**

A: Replace toys when they show significant wear, damage, or become too small for your growing puppy. Signs it's time to replace include visible cracks, pieces breaking off, the toy becoming small enough to swallow whole, excessive fraying of rope toys, or if your puppy has outgrown the size. Regularly inspect toys for damage—most should last through the teething phase (3-6 months), but heavily used toys may need more frequent replacement.

**Q: Are ice cubes safe for teething puppies?**

A: Ice cubes can provide temporary relief but come with significant risks. They're too hard for baby teeth (can crack or damage them), create a slip-and-puck hazard that can cause falls, and can become stuck in a puppy's mouth or throat. Instead, use frozen wet washcloths, frozen puppy-safe rubber chews, or frozen treats specifically designed for dogs. These provide the cold relief without the hardness and choking risks of ice cubes.

**Q: When should I transition from puppy chews to adult chews?**

A: The transition depends on when your puppy's adult teeth finish coming in—usually by 6-7 months. You can start introducing slightly harder adult-appropriate toys gradually as adult teeth emerge, but keep some softer puppy toys available until the full set of adult teeth is in place (around 7-9 months). Monitor your puppy's chewing behavior and gum condition during transition. If they're comfortable with harder toys and no longer showing teething discomfort, they're ready for adult chews.

**Q: Do teething chews help with destructive chewing behavior?**

A: Yes, providing appropriate chew toys can significantly reduce destructive chewing behavior. Teething puppies have an innate chewing urge and will chew on whatever they can find for relief. By offering satisfying alternatives—especially ones that address pain relief—you redirect that natural behavior away from inappropriate items (shoes, furniture, household items). However, you still need to manage the environment and supervise until your puppy understands what's appropriate to chew.

**Q: Can teething toys cause digestive issues if swallowed?**

A: It depends on the toy material and amount swallowed. Small pieces of rubber, nylon, or other synthetic materials can cause intestinal blockages, especially in puppies. Rope fibers can potentially cause linear foreign bodies or tie up parts of the intestine. Always supervise chewing sessions, regularly inspect toys for damage, and remove toys that are breaking down. If your puppy swallows a significant piece of toy or shows signs of digestive distress (vomiting, loss of appetite, lethargy), seek veterinary care immediately.
